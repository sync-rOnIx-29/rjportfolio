Palawan

The last ecological frontier


Palawan, known as the "Last Ecological Frontier" of the Philippines, is an archipelago province situated in the MIMAROPA region. This stunning province stretches from Mindoro in the northeast all the way to Borneo in the southwest, encompassing a diverse landscape of islands, pristine beaches, towering limestone cliffs, and lush rainforests. Its strategic location has made it a crossroads of cultures and a vital ecological sanctuary.


Palawan's history spans over 50,000 years, with influences from Malay, Chinese, Indian, and Spanish civilizations. Initially inhabited by indigenous populations, it became a hub for trade and cultural exchange. Palawan played a significant role in World War II and is now a renowned tourist destination.


The instant I arrived, I was mesmerized by the sheer beauty of its scenery. Precipitous limestone cliffs in El Nido rose defiantly against the skyline, their jagged tips softened by the emerald-colored waters below. I stood transfixed as the sun colored the sky pink and gold, glinting off the crystal-clear lagoons that looked almost too perfect to exist.
